Understanding addiction — campaign plan

Key terms, myths, causes, case study analysis, healthy coping, getting help, and a link to the campaign slides.

Phase 1

1. Key terms

Substance abuse

Substance abuse is when someone uses drugs or alcohol in a harmful way or too much, even when it causes problems in their life, health, school, or relationships.

Dependence

Dependence happens when a person’s body or mind feels like it needs a substance in order to function normally.

Withdrawal

Withdrawal is the uncomfortable or painful feelings a person may experience when they stop using a substance, such as headaches, anxiety, shaking, nausea, or sadness.

2. Myths about addiction

Common myths

  • Addiction is a choice.
  • You have to use a substance every day to be addicted.
  • Addiction only happens to certain people.

Main myth to debunk

“Addiction is a choice.”

The truth

Addiction is not simply a choice. While someone may choose to try a substance at first, repeated use can change how the brain works. Over time, the brain can become dependent on the substance, making it very difficult to stop without help and support.

3. Causes of addiction

Biological factors

  • Genetics (addiction can run in families)
  • Changes in the brain caused by substance use

Psychological factors

  • Stress
  • Anxiety or depression
  • Wanting to escape problems or difficult emotions

Social factors

  • Peer pressure and influence from friends
  • Home, school, or community environment
  • Media and social media influence

4. Case study analysis

How did the person start?

The person may have started using substances because of curiosity, stress, or pressure from friends.

What influenced them?

Their choices may have been influenced by:

  • Peer pressure
  • Problems at home or school
  • Stress, anxiety, or emotional struggles

How did it become an addiction?

At first, the person used substances to feel better or fit in. Over time, their brain became used to the substance, and eventually they found it difficult to stop using it.

Phase 2

5. Healthy coping strategies

Exercise

Exercise helps reduce stress, improve mood, and increase energy. Staying active keeps both the mind and body healthy, lowering the chances of turning to harmful habits.

Talking to someone

Talking to a trusted friend, parent, teacher, or counselor can help people manage their problems instead of keeping emotions bottled up inside.

Mindfulness

Mindfulness activities such as deep breathing, meditation, or journaling help people stay calm and manage emotions in healthy ways.

6. Getting help

Who can someone talk to?

  • A trusted adult
  • A parent or guardian
  • A teacher
  • A school counselor
  • A close friend or mentor

Why is it important to get help?

Getting help is important because addiction can become more serious over time if someone struggles alone. Support from others can provide guidance, encouragement, and healthier ways to cope with challenges.
