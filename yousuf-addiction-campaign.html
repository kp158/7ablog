Understanding addiction — campaign plan

Key terms, myths, causes, coping, help, campaign planning, storyboard, and a link to the slide deck (Phases 1–3).

Phase 1

1. Key terms

Substance abuse:

When someone uses drugs or alcohol in a harmful way or too much, even when it causes problems.

Dependence:

When your body or mind feels like it needs a substance to function normally.

Withdrawal:

The uncomfortable or painful feelings someone gets when they stop using a substance (like headaches, anxiety, or nausea).

2. Myths about addiction

3 myths:

  • Addiction is a choice.
  • You have to use it every day to be addicted.
  • Addiction only happens to certain people.

Main myth to focus on: Addiction is a choice.

Truth:

Addiction is not just a choice, it changes how the brain works. People may choose to try something at first, but over time it becomes hard to stop because the brain becomes dependent on it.

3. Causes of addiction

Biological factors:

  • Genetics (runs in families)
  • Brain changes from using substances

Psychological factors:

  • Stress
  • Anxiety or depression
  • Wanting to escape problems

Social factors:

  • Peer pressure (friends influence)
  • Environment (home, school, community)
  • Media influence

4. Case study analysis

How did the person start?

They started using substances because of friends or curiosity.

What influenced them?

Peer pressure, stress, or problems at home/school.

How it became addiction?

They kept using it to feel better, their brain got used to it, and eventually they couldn’t stop.

Phase 2

5. Healthy coping strategies

Exercise

Exercise helps reduce stress and improves your mood. Staying active keeps your mind and body busy so you are less likely to turn to unhealthy habits like addiction.

Talking to someone

Talking to a trusted person like a friend, parent, or teacher helps you deal with problems instead of keeping them inside. This can prevent people from using harmful behaviors to cope.

Mindfulness

Mindfulness, like deep breathing, helps you stay calm and control your emotions. It makes it easier to make better choices instead of turning to addiction.

6. Getting help

Who can someone talk to?

  • A trusted adult
  • A teacher
  • A school counselor
  • A parent or guardian

Why is it important to get help?

Getting help is important because addiction can get worse if someone deals with it alone. Talking to others gives support, advice, and helps people feel less alone. It also helps them make better decisions.

7. Campaign planning

  • Target audience: Grade 7 students
  • Key message: Addiction can affect anyone, but there are healthy ways to cope and get help.
  • Main myth to debunk: Only adults get addicted
  • Media formats: Slides and video

8. Storyboard / plan

Slides:

The slides will explain what addiction is, how it can start, healthy coping strategies, and where to get help.

Poster:

The poster will include a short message, 2–3 coping strategies, and a reminder to get help.

Video:

The video will explain addiction in a simple way, give an example, and encourage students to talk to someone.

How all parts connect:

All parts share the same message about understanding addiction, using healthy coping strategies, and getting help when needed.
