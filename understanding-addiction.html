Understanding Addiction: You Are Not Weak

What is drug addiction?

Drug addiction is a substance use disorder that causes a person to repeatedly use harmful drugs or substances even when it affects their health, emotions, and daily life. Addiction changes the brain and can make it difficult for someone to stop using substances without help.

Key terms

What does substance abuse mean?

Substance abuse means using drugs or alcohol in a harmful way that can affect your health, relationships, school life, or behavior.

What is dependence?

Dependence is when a person relies on a drug or substance because their body or mind feels like it needs it to function normally.

What is withdrawal?

Withdrawal happens when someone stops using a drug or uses less of it and experiences uncomfortable symptoms such as anxiety, restlessness, headaches, or sickness.

Myth about addiction

Myth:

“People who struggle with substance use are weak and lack strength.”

Truth:

Addiction is not about being weak. Many people develop addictions because of stress, mental health struggles, peer pressure, family environment, or changes in the brain caused by substances. Addiction is a health issue, and people can recover with support and healthy choices.

Causes of addiction

Biological factors

  • Some people have family members who struggle with addiction, which can increase their own risk.
  • Addictive substances affect the brain by releasing large amounts of dopamine, a chemical linked to pleasure and reward.
  • Over time, the brain may begin to depend on the substance.

Psychological factors

  • People may start using substances because of depression, anxiety, stress, or trauma.
  • Low self-esteem or emotional struggles can also increase risk.
  • Starting drug use at a young age increases the chance of addiction later in life.

Social factors

  • Growing up around family members who use drugs or alcohol can increase risk.
  • Peer pressure from friends or classmates may encourage someone to try substances.
  • Feeling left out or wanting to fit in can influence decisions.

Case study: August’s story

August moved to a new city and felt lonely because he did not have many friends. Eventually, he joined a group that made him feel included. Some of his new friends used substances and vapes, and they pressured him to try them too.

At first, August used substances to fit in and reduce his anxiety. Over time, he began using more because the substances made him feel calmer. Eventually, he became dependent on them. When he did not use the substance, he felt restless and uncomfortable, which are signs of withdrawal.

This case study shows how social pressure, anxiety, and emotional struggles can lead to addiction.

Healthy coping strategies

1. Exercise and physical activities

Exercise helps reduce stress and improves mood. Activities like sports, walking, or working out can distract people from unhealthy habits and improve mental health.

2. Talking to someone

Speaking with a trusted adult, friend, counselor, or teacher can help people deal with stress and emotions instead of turning to substances.

3. Healthy hobbies

Listening to music, drawing, gaming in moderation, reading, or joining clubs can keep people busy and focused on positive activities.

4. Avoiding risky situations

Staying away from places or people connected to drugs can help prevent relapse and reduce temptation.

5. Setting goals

Creating a quit date or personal goal can motivate someone to work toward recovery step by step.

Getting help is important

Nobody has to face addiction alone. Asking for help is a strong and brave decision.

Trusted people to talk to

  • Parents or guardians
  • Teachers
  • School counselors
  • Doctors
  • Trusted relatives or friends

Final message

Addiction does not mean someone is weak. Many factors such as stress, mental health, family environment, and peer pressure can contribute to substance use. The good news is that recovery is possible. Healthy coping strategies, supportive friends and family, and professional help can make a huge difference.
